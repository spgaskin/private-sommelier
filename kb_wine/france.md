# France — Wine Regions

## Bordeaux
Southwest France, on the Gironde estuary. Blended reds dominate. Left Bank (Médoc,
Graves) is Cabernet Sauvignon-led, gravel soils, structured and tannic, ages for
decades. Right Bank (Saint-Émilion, Pomerol) is Merlot-led, plusher and earlier-drinking.
Classic blend: Cabernet Sauvignon, Merlot, Cabernet Franc, plus Petit Verdot and Malbec.
The 1855 Classification ranks Left Bank châteaux into five growths (First Growths /
Premiers Crus: Lafite Rothschild, Latour, Margaux, Haut-Brion, and Mouton Rothschild,
elevated in 1973). Dry whites from Sauvignon Blanc + Sémillon (Pessac-Léognan). Sweet
botrytized whites from Sauternes and Barsac (Château d'Yquem is the benchmark).

## Burgundy (Bourgogne)
East-central France. Almost entirely single-variety: Pinot Noir for reds, Chardonnay
for whites. Terroir-obsessed: tiny climats ranked Regional < Village < Premier Cru <
Grand Cru. Côte de Nuits (Gevrey-Chambertin, Vosne-Romanée, Chambolle-Musigny) for the
greatest reds; Côte de Beaune (Puligny-Montrachet, Meursault, Corton) for the greatest
whites. Chablis (cool, northern) makes lean, mineral, unoaked Chardonnay. Beaujolais
(south) makes light, juicy reds from Gamay; ten named Crus (Morgon, Fleurie, Moulin-à-Vent).

## Champagne
Cool northern region; the benchmark for traditional-method sparkling wine (secondary
fermentation in the bottle). Grapes: Chardonnay, Pinot Noir, Pinot Meunier. Styles by
sweetness: Brut Nature/Zero, Extra Brut, Brut (most common), Extra Dry, Sec, Demi-Sec.
Non-Vintage (NV) blends house style across years; Vintage is single great years. Blanc de
Blancs = all Chardonnay; Blanc de Noirs = black grapes only. Only sparkling wine from this
region may legally be called Champagne.

## Rhône Valley
Northern Rhône: Syrah for reds (Côte-Rôtie, Hermitage, Cornas), Viognier for whites
(Condrieu). Southern Rhône: blends led by Grenache with Syrah and Mourvèdre (the "GSM"),
warmer and fruitier; Châteauneuf-du-Pape permits up to 13 varieties. Côtes du Rhône is
the everyday regional appellation.

## Loire Valley
Cool, varied. Sauvignon Blanc in Sancerre and Pouilly-Fumé (flinty, crisp). Chenin Blanc
in Vouvray and Savennières (dry to sweet, age-worthy). Cabernet Franc reds in Chinon and
Bourgueil. Muscadet (Melon de Bourgogne) near the Atlantic, often sur lie.

## Alsace
Northeast, Germanic influence, mostly aromatic whites labeled by variety: Riesling,
Gewürztraminer, Pinot Gris, Muscat. Typically dry, but late-harvest styles exist
(Vendanges Tardives). Tall flute bottles.
